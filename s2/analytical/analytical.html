Unit Digit Concept

The unit digit is the right-most digit of a number. When calculating the unit digit of a product or a number raised to a power, you often only need to consider the unit digits of the numbers involved.

Product Type Questions

To find the unit digit of a product of several numbers, multiply only their unit digits.

Divisibility Rules

Divisibility rules are shortcuts to determine if a number can be evenly divided by another, and they can also help in finding remainders.

Rules for Powers of 2 (2, 4, 8, 16, etc.)

The rule is based on the number of last digits.

  • Divisibility by 2: The last digit must be divisible by 2.
  • Divisibility by 4: The last two digits must be divisible by 4.
  • Divisibility by 8: The last three digits must be divisible by 8.

Rules for Powers of 5 (5, 25, 125, etc.)

This follows a similar pattern to the powers of 2.

  • Divisibility by 5: The last digit must be 0 or 5.
  • Divisibility by 25: The last two digits must be divisible by 25 (e.g., 00, 25, 50, 75).
  • Divisibility by 125: The last three digits must be divisible by 125.

Rules for 3 and 9

These rules are based on the sum of the digits.

  • Divisibility by 3: The sum of all digits must be divisible by 3.

  • Divisibility by 9: The sum of all digits must be divisible by 9.

    • Shortcut for 9: Pair up digits that sum to 9 and cancel them out. If all digits are cancelled, the number is divisible by 9.

Rule for 6

A number is divisible by 6 if it is divisible by both 2 and 3.

Rule for 11

This rule uses the alternating sum of digits.

  1. Sum the digits in the odd positions (1st, 3rd, 5th, etc.).
  2. Sum the digits in the even positions (2nd, 4th, 6th, etc.).
  3. Find the difference between the two sums.
  4. If the difference is 0 or a multiple of 11, the number is divisible by 11.

  • Example: For the number 1331:

    • Odd positions sum: 1 + 3 = 4.
    • Even positions sum: 3 + 1 = 4.
    • Difference: 4 - 4 = 0. The number is divisible by 11.

Classification of Numbers

Numbers can be broadly classified into two main types: real numbers and imaginary numbers. For aptitude tests, the focus is solely on real numbers.

Real Numbers

Any number that can be represented on a number line is a real number. This includes:

  • Positive and negative integers
  • Zero
  • Fractions (e.g., 1/10)
  • Decimals (e.g., 0.5)
  • Root values (e.g., âˆš5), which can be plotted using geometric principles like the Pythagorean theorem.
Imaginary Numbers

Numbers that cannot be represented on a number line are imaginary. An example is iota (i), which is the square root of -1. These are generally not part of the aptitude syllabus.

Sub-classification of Real Numbers

Real numbers can be further divided into rational and irrational numbers.

Rational Numbers

A number is rational if it can be expressed in the p/q form, where q is not equal to zero.

  • Integers: All integers are rational numbers (e.g., 2 can be written as 2/1).
  • Fractions: These are inherently in p/q form and are rational.
  • Terminating Decimals: Decimals that end, like 0.5, are rational because they can be converted to a fraction (0.5 = 5/10).
  • Non-Terminating, Repeating Decimals: Decimals that continue infinitely but have a repeating pattern, like 0.333..., are also rational as they can be converted to a fraction (0.333... = 1/3).
Irrational Numbers

A number is irrational if it cannot be expressed in the p/q form.

  • These are non-terminating and non-repeating decimals.
  • Examples include root values like âˆš2, âˆš3, and special numbers like Pi (Ï€) and Euler's number (e).

Integers

Integers can be classified in several ways:

  • By Sign: Positive integers (1, 2, 3,...), negative integers (-1, -2, -3,...), and zero.
  • Even and Odd:
    • Even Numbers: Divisible by 2.
    • Odd Numbers: Not divisible by 2.
    • Zero (0) is an even number because it is divisible by 2 (0/2 = 0) and it falls between two odd numbers (-1 and 1).
  • Prime and Composite:
    • Prime Numbers: Have exactly two factors: 1 and the number itself (e.g., 2, 3, 5).
    • Composite Numbers: Have more than two factors.
    • One (1) is neither prime nor composite as it only has one factor.

Special Number Types

Co-prime Numbers

Also known as relatively prime numbers, these are a set of two or more numbers whose only common factor is 1 (their Highest Common Factor is 1). The numbers themselves do not have to be prime. For example, 3 and 10 are co-prime.

Perfect Numbers

A perfect number is a positive integer that is equal to the sum of all its factors, excluding the number itself.

  • Example (6): The factors of 6 are 1, 2, 3, and 6. The sum of its factors excluding itself is 1 + 2 + 3 = 6.
  • The first three perfect numbers to remember for exams are 6, 28, and 496.

Conversion of Decimals to Fractions

Converting Terminating Decimals

Terminating decimals, which are decimals that end, can be converted to fractions using a straightforward method.

Standard Method

  1. Write the decimal as a fraction by placing it over 1. For example, 0.75 becomes .

  2. Multiply both the numerator and the denominator by a power of 10. The number of zeros should match the number of digits after the decimal point. For 0.75, there are two digits after the decimal, so you multiply by 100.

  3. Simplify the resulting fraction to its lowest terms.

Converting Repeating Decimals

For non-terminating, repeating decimals, there are both a traditional algebraic method and a shortcut method useful for competitive exams.

Normal (Algebraic) Method

This method uses algebra to find the fractional equivalent. Let's use the example 0.333....

  1. Set the repeating decimal equal to a variable, such as x.

  2. Multiply both sides by a power of 10 to shift the decimal point. Since one digit is repeating, we multiply by 10.

  3. Subtract the first equation from the second to eliminate the repeating part.

  4. Solve for x and simplify the fraction.

A factorial is the product of all positive integers up to a given number (n!). For example, 5! = 5 Ã— 4 Ã— 3 Ã— 2 Ã— 1. The main type of question related to factorials involves finding the number of trailing zeros in its expanded value.

The "AND/OR" Concept (Multiplication vs. Addition)

The core idea is to determine when to multiply and when to add probabilities or possibilities.

  • Multiplication (AND): Use when the work or task is in progress and not yet completed. Each step contributes to the completion of a single overall task.

    • Example: Traveling from Jalandhar to Delhi
      • To reach Ludhiana from Jalandhar, there are ways (train, bus, car).
      • To reach Delhi from Ludhiana, there are ways (train, bus).
      • Since the task is not complete after reaching Ludhiana, multiply: total ways.

  • Addition (OR): Use when the work or task is completed, and there's an alternative way to achieve it. This accounts for different paths that complete the same goal independently.

    • Example: Continuing with Traveling from Jalandhar to Delhi
      • Suppose there are direct routes from Jalandhar to Delhi:
        • Direct train: way.
        • Direct bus: way.
      • Total ways when including Ludhiana route (from earlier): total ways.

Permutation and Combination Definitions

  • Permutation is used when the order of selection matters. For example, arranging items or selecting a president and vice-president where order is important.
  • Combination is used when the order of selection does not matter. For example, selecting a committee where roles are not distinct.
